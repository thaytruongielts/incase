Slide 1: Using 'In case'

We use **in case** to say "because it is possible that something will happen." It's about a precautionary action.

  • Your car should have a spare wheel **in case** you have a puncture. (You do this because a puncture is a possibility.)
  • I'll leave my phone on **in case** Jane calls.
  • I took an umbrella **in case** it rained.
**Tip:** 'In case' is followed by a **subject + verb**. It often refers to a future or present possibility.

Slide 2: 'In case' vs. 'If'

These two words have different meanings. Use **in case** for a **precautionary** action. Use **if** for a **conditional** action.

  • We'll buy some more food **in case** Tom comes. (We will buy food now because his coming is a possibility.)
  • We'll buy some more food **if** Tom comes. (We will wait for Tom to come, and then we will buy the food.)
The action you are taking with 'in case' happens *before* the other event. With 'if', the action happens *after* the other event.

Slide 3: 'In case' with Past Tense

You can use **in case** with a past tense verb to say why someone did something in the past.

  • I left my phone switched on **in case** Jane called.
  • I wrote down the name of the book **in case** I forgot it.

Slide 4: Using 'In case of'

We use **in case of** before a **noun** to mean "if there is a..." or "in the event of a...". This is often used for emergencies or instructions.

  • **In case of** fire, use the stairs.
  • **In case of** emergency, telephone this number.
  • He was advised to get insurance **in case of** illness.
